About

Eddie Reynolds is an American guitarist and songwriter from Yonkers, New York, whose career spans from the raw club scene of the 1960s to the early solo years of Lou Reed and into a later life of independent songwriting and creative collaboration.

Reynolds began playing guitar in the 1960s, performing with local bands including The Odds and Ends alongside Ralph Massella. After graduating from Gorton High School in 1969, he became a regular presence in Yonkers’ North End music scene, where he developed both his guitar style and his voice as a songwriter. Unlike many bar-band musicians of the time, Reynolds was already writing original material—songs shaped by the working-class environment and street-level realism of his surroundings.

He emerged as a key creative force in The Tots, a Yonkers-based band built around a powerful twin-guitar sound shared with Vinny LaPorta. Alongside Bobby Resigno, Scottie Clark, and vocalist Rickie Penna, Reynolds helped define the band’s identity, contributing original songs that set them apart from the typical cover-driven circuit.

In 1972, The Tots were selected by Lou Reed as his first solo touring band following the Velvet Underground. Reynolds toured extensively with Reed in support of his debut solo album and Transformer, helping translate Reed’s music into a more grounded, hard-driving live sound. Their work during this period is captured on American Poet, recorded in December 1972, and in Reed’s first major New York City solo performance at Alice Tully Hall in January 1973.

Over time, the band evolved from an inexperienced group into a tight, dynamic unit known for its “funky” twin-guitar interplay. However, in April 1973, as Reed’s vision shifted toward the darker and more orchestrated direction of Berlin, he replaced The Tots mid-tour with a more seasoned lineup.

Following their departure from the national stage, The Tots returned to the Yonkers and Westchester music scene, where they remained a respected regional force. Though often labeled a “no-name” bar band, they were later recognized as an underrated and unfairly maligned group whose raw, authentic sound stood in contrast to more polished acts of the era.

Throughout these years, Reynolds continued writing and performing, maintaining his identity not just as a guitarist, but as a songwriter. In later decades, he transitioned into a more independent phase, releasing original material and sharing his work through platforms such as CD Baby and SoundCloud.

His recent work is also featured in The Eddie Reynolds “Song Writer” Hang Out, a curated YouTube playlist created in collaboration with longtime friend and visual artist Ed Murphy. The project combines Reynolds’ original songs with Murphy’s video production, presenting a more intimate and reflective side of his music while extending his creative reach into the digital era.

From the clubs of Yonkers to the stages of early 1970s rock and into a lasting independent career, Eddie Reynolds represents a continuous thread in American rock music—one rooted not in fame, but in authenticity, songwriting, and the enduring drive to create.
